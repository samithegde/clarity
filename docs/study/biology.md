# Cellular Respiration and Mitochondria

## Mitochondria

Mitochondria are organelles found in most eukaryotic cells. They are often called the **powerhouses of the cell** because they generate most of the cell's supply of adenosine triphosphate (ATP), used as a source of chemical energy.

## Role in Cellular Respiration

Mitochondria carry out **aerobic respiration**, a process that converts glucose and oxygen into ATP, carbon dioxide, and water. The overall reaction can be summarized as:

Glucose + Oxygen → ATP + Carbon Dioxide + Water

### Key stages (inside the mitochondrion)

1. **Krebs cycle (citric acid cycle)** — occurs in the mitochondrial matrix; breaks down acetyl-CoA and produces electron carriers (NADH, FADH₂).
2. **Electron transport chain** — located on the inner mitochondrial membrane; uses electrons from NADH and FADH₂ to pump protons and drive ATP synthase.

## Why ATP Matters

ATP stores energy in its phosphate bonds. When a cell needs energy for muscle contraction, active transport, or biosynthesis, it hydrolyzes ATP to ADP + phosphate, releasing usable energy.

## Visual identification

In typical textbook cell diagrams, mitochondria appear as **oval or sausage-shaped structures** with a folded inner membrane (cristae). They are usually drawn near the center of the cell, distinct from the nucleus and ribosomes.
